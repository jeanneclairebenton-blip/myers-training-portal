Math 101 — Comps, ROI & ARV

⏱ 10 min 🏆 Milestone: Comp Analyst

💰 Why This Matters

This is the math that turns "I found a house" into "$50K profit for your investor." If you can't run comps and determine ARV, you can't evaluate deals — and you can't make money.

📖 LEARN

CMA Best Practices

A Comparative Market Analysis (CMA) is how you determine what a property is worth after repairs. Here are the rules:

📅
Last 6 Months

Only use properties sold within the last 6 months. Older data = bad data.

🛣️
Don't Cross Major Roads

Highways, interstates, and major thoroughfares create different neighborhoods.

🏘️
Stay in the Subdivision

Same neighborhood, same school district, same feel.

🏗️
Like Year & Structure

Compare similar age, style, and construction type.

3️⃣
Minimum 3 Comps

Always use at least 3 comparable properties. More is better.

📐
Peg Comp Selection

Match sqft, bed/bath, and lot size as closely as possible.

Determining ARV from Comps

After-Repair Value (ARV) is what the property will be worth after all repairs are completed. Here's how to determine it:

ARV = Average of your best comparable sold prices Select 3-5 comps that match your subject property post-renovation. Weight comps that are most similar (same street, same style) more heavily. Adjust for differences in sqft, condition, and features.

Sample CMA

Property SqFt Bed/Bath Year Sold Price $/SqFt
🎯 Subject Property 1,450 3/2 1978 TBD —
Comp 1 — 123 Oak St 1,500 3/2 1976 $195,000 $130
Comp 2 — 456 Elm St 1,400 3/2 1980 $188,000 $134
Comp 3 — 789 Pine St 1,475 3/2 1979 $192,000 $130
// ARV Determination
Average Sold Price = ($195K + $188K + $192K) ÷ 3
ARV = $191,667 → Round to $190,000 (conservative)
